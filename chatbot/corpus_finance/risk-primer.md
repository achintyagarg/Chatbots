# Portfolio Risk Primer

Reference notes on core wealth-management and risk concepts, in the
plain-language form I want the assistant to use when explaining them.

## Diversification

Diversification reduces risk only to the extent that holdings are imperfectly
correlated. Ten tech growth stocks are one bet wearing ten tickers: their
pairwise correlations in a drawdown approach 1 exactly when diversification
is needed most. Measure diversification by the correlation structure, not by
the count of positions.

## Volatility versus risk

Volatility is a statistical description of how much returns wiggle. Risk, for
an individual, is the chance of not meeting a real objective -- retirement
income, a house purchase -- or of being forced to sell at a bad time.
Volatility is a useful proxy only when the horizon is short or leverage is
involved. A 30-year investor's dominant risks are inflation and abandoning
the plan at the bottom, not daily variance.

## Drawdowns and recovery math

A 25% drawdown needs a 33% gain to recover; a 50% drawdown needs 100%.
This asymmetry is why sizing to survivable drawdowns matters more than
squeezing expected return. The question to ask of any allocation: if it
halves, do I keep following the plan?

## Sharpe ratio, honestly

Sharpe is excess return per unit of volatility. It is comparable across
strategies only over the same period and frequency, it says nothing about
tail shape, and it flatters strategies that sell insurance -- steady small
gains punctuated by rare large losses. Never compare a live Sharpe to a
backtest Sharpe as if they were the same kind of number.

## Rebalancing

Rebalancing is a risk-control mechanism, not a return enhancer. Its expected
contribution to return is modest and regime-dependent; its contribution to
keeping the portfolio at the intended risk level is structural. Calendar
rebalancing (quarterly or semiannual) captures most of the benefit with the
least monitoring burden.
